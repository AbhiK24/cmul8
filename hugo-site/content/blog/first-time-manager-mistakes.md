Getting promoted to manager feels like winning. Then reality hits.

You're suddenly responsible for other people's careers. You have conversations you never had to have before. And the skills that got you promoted are mostly useless now.

I've watched dozens of new managers make the same mistakes. Here's what to avoid.

## Mistake 1: Trying to Stay Buddies

You got promoted. Your former peers now report to you. You want everything to stay the same, so you downplay your authority, avoid hard conversations, and pretend you're still just one of the team.

This never works. Eventually you'll need to give critical feedback, make an unpopular decision, or know something you can't share. The friendship you're protecting becomes an obstacle.

**The fix:** Acknowledge the change directly. "Our relationship is different now. I'll need to give you feedback and make decisions you might not like. That doesn't mean I don't respect you—it means I'm doing my job."

Awkward? Yes. Necessary? Absolutely.

## Mistake 2: Solving Everyone's Problems

Your report comes to you with a problem. Your instinct is to solve it—you probably know the answer faster than they'd figure it out.

But you're not paid to solve problems anymore. You're paid to develop people who can solve problems.

Every time you give the answer, you're training them to come to you instead of thinking for themselves. Eventually you become a bottleneck, and they stay dependent.

**The fix:** Ask questions instead.
- "What have you tried?"
- "What do you think we should do?"
- "What's the worst that could happen if you just decide?"

Yes, this takes longer. That's the point.

## Mistake 3: Avoiding Feedback Until It's Too Late

This is the classic. Something bothers you about a report's work. You let it slide because it's not a big deal. It happens again. And again. Now it's a pattern, and you've never said anything.

When you finally bring it up, they're blindsided. "Why didn't you tell me sooner?"

Good question.

**The fix:** Give feedback when it's small. "Hey, I noticed you interrupted Sarah in that meeting. Just something to watch." That's it. Quick, specific, in the moment.

If you wait until you're frustrated, you've already failed them.

## Mistake 4: Treating Everyone the Same

Fair doesn't mean equal. Different people need different things.

Your senior report who's been here 5 years needs autonomy and occasional check-ins. Your new hire needs structure, frequent feedback, and probably some hand-holding. Managing them identically means one of them is getting what they don't need.

**The fix:** Ask them directly. "How do you like to receive feedback? How much context do you need on projects? How often should we check in?"

Then actually adjust your approach.

## Mistake 5: Getting Credit and Blame Wrong

New managers often do both backwards.

When things go well: "I made this happen."
When things go badly: "My team screwed up."

Your reports notice. And they stop trusting you.

**The fix:** Flip it.
- Success: "The team did this. Here's what Sarah and James did specifically."
- Failure: "I own this. Here's what I'm doing to fix it."

Protecting your team and sharing credit costs you nothing, but builds loyalty that pays dividends for years.

## The Meta-Mistake

Here's the thing nobody tells new managers: **your job fundamentally changed, but nobody gave you new skills.**

Being a great individual contributor got you promoted. But management is a different craft. The skills overlap less than you think.

You're going to mess up. The managers who grow are the ones who admit it, learn from it, and keep showing up.

The ones who struggle? They never accept that the game changed.
