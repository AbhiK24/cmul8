Here's an uncomfortable truth most managers avoid: your employees actually want more negative feedback, not less.

A [Harvard Business Review study](https://hbr.org/) found that 72% of employees believe their performance would improve if managers gave more corrective feedback. Even more surprising—employees reported wanting *less* positive feedback and *more* negative feedback than they were getting.

So why does giving negative feedback feel so hard?

Because most of us do it wrong. We wait too long, soften it until it's meaningless, or deliver it in ways that damage trust instead of building it.

Let's fix that.

## The SBII Framework: Feedback That Actually Works

Forget the outdated feedback sandwich. Here's a framework that's direct, respectful, and actionable:

### S — Situation
Get specific about when and where. Not "lately" or "sometimes"—pick one clear instance.

> "In yesterday's client presentation at 2pm..."

This anchors the conversation in observable reality, not vague impressions.

### B — Behavior
Describe exactly what you observed. Not your interpretation. Not what you think they were thinking. Just what happened.

> "...you interrupted Sarah three times while she was explaining the timeline, and spoke over her when she tried to continue."

The key word is *observed*. If you can't point to specific actions, you're not ready to give feedback.

### I — Impact
Explain the concrete effect. On you, on the team, on the work, on the business.

> "The client looked confused about who was leading the project. Sarah seemed frustrated and stopped contributing. I'm worried it undermined our credibility with the account."

Impact makes abstract behavior feel real and consequential.

### I — Invite
This is what most frameworks miss. After delivering your perspective, genuinely ask for theirs.

> "What was going on for you in that moment?"

You might discover the interrupting came from nervousness, not arrogance. Or they had information you didn't. This changes how you solve it together.

## The 48-Hour Rule

Feedback has a shelf life. Deliver it within 48 hours or it loses most of its value.

**Why timing matters:**
- Details stay sharp for both of you
- The behavior is still correctable in context
- Waiting builds resentment (yours) and blindsides them (theirs)
- Patterns are easier to address when caught early

The one exception: don't give feedback when you're still angry. Wait until you can be constructive—but don't wait so long you're now having a different conversation.

## What Not to Say (With Alternatives)

| Instead of this... | Say this... |
|-------------------|-------------|
| "You always miss deadlines" | "The last three reports were submitted after the deadline" |
| "You need to be more professional" | "When you checked your phone during the client meeting, they paused and seemed distracted" |
| "That presentation was bad" | "The presentation didn't include the competitive analysis the exec team asked for" |
| "You're not a team player" | "When you left the standup early yesterday, the team couldn't get your input on the blocking issue" |
| "You should have known better" | "What information would have helped you make a different call?" |

Notice the pattern: **behaviors and specifics, not labels and judgments**.

## The Part Most People Mess Up

After delivering feedback, most managers keep talking. They over-explain, justify, soften, apologize.

Stop. Let silence do its work.

Give them space to respond. They might:

- **Agree immediately** — Great, move to problem-solving
- **Get defensive** — Acknowledge, then redirect to impact
- **Offer context you didn't have** — Listen. You might learn something important
- **Deny it happened** — Stick to what you observed without arguing

Whatever their reaction, stay curious. Your job isn't to win—it's to be heard, understand their perspective, and find a path forward together.

## End With Forward Motion

Don't leave the conversation hanging in the problem. Move to action.

> "What do you think would help prevent this next time?"
> "What support would be useful here?"
> "How do you want to handle this going forward?"

Notice these are questions, not commands. You're collaborating on a solution, not dictating one. People commit more strongly to plans they helped create.

And always—always—end by expressing confidence in their ability to improve:

> "I'm bringing this up because I know you can do better, and I want to see you succeed here."

This isn't empty praise. It's the belief that makes feedback worth giving in the first place.

## Five Mistakes That Destroy Trust

**1. Waiting too long**
Feedback delivered months later feels like an ambush. "Why didn't you tell me sooner?" is a fair question you won't have a good answer to.

**2. Doing it publicly**
Even mild criticism in front of others is humiliating. Always private. Always.

**3. Making it about them, not the behavior**
"You're disorganized" attacks their identity. "The project plan didn't include milestones or owners" addresses something they can fix.

**4. Guessing at intent**
You don't know why they did what they did. "You clearly don't care about quality" is an assumption that will blow up the conversation. Ask instead.

**5. Apologizing for giving feedback**
"I'm sorry to have to tell you this..." undermines your message before you've delivered it. You're not doing something wrong. You're doing your job.

## The Research Is Clear

According to research published in [Harvard Business Review](https://professional.dce.harvard.edu/blog/how-to-give-negative-feedback-to-employees/):

- **72%** of employees say their performance would improve with more corrective feedback
- Employees actually want **less** positive feedback and **more** constructive criticism than they typically receive
- Feedback focused on behavior (not character) is significantly more likely to produce behavior change

The problem isn't that people can't handle negative feedback. The problem is that most negative feedback is delivered badly.

## A Script You Can Use Today

Here's everything put together:

> "Hey, do you have a few minutes? I want to talk about what happened in yesterday's client meeting.
>
> When you were presenting the timeline, I noticed you spoke over Sarah a few times when she tried to add context. The client seemed confused about who was leading, and Sarah looked frustrated and stopped contributing.
>
> I'm not saying you did it intentionally—I'm curious what was happening for you in that moment.
>
> *[Listen to their response]*
>
> That makes sense. Going forward, how do you think we could make sure everyone gets space to contribute in client meetings?
>
> *[Collaborate on solution]*
>
> Great. I appreciate you hearing this. I'm bringing it up because I know you're capable of running these meetings well, and I want the client to see that too."

That's it. Direct, specific, curious, collaborative.

## The Real Skill

Here's what nobody tells you: giving feedback gets easier with practice, but it never feels *good*. If it did, you'd probably be doing it wrong.

The discomfort you feel? That's you caring about the person and the relationship. Channel it into being thoughtful and specific, not into avoiding the conversation entirely.

Your job isn't to make people feel good in the moment. It's to help them grow. Sometimes that means short-term discomfort for long-term development.

The managers who avoid hard conversations aren't being kind. They're being cowardly. And their reports pay the price—often finding out too late that there was a problem nobody told them about.

Don't be that manager.
