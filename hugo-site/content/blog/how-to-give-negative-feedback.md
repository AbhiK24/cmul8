Look, nobody enjoys giving negative feedback. But here's the thing most people get wrong: avoiding the conversation isn't being nice. It's being cowardly. And it hurts the person more in the long run.

I've watched managers let issues fester for months because they "didn't want to hurt feelings." Then they're shocked when they have to fire someone who never got a real chance to improve.

Let's fix that.

## The Framework That Actually Works

Forget the feedback sandwich. It's manipulative and everyone sees through it. Instead, use **SBI: Situation, Behavior, Impact.**

### Situation
Get specific about when and where. Not "lately" or "sometimes." Pick one instance.

> "In yesterday's client presentation..."

### Behavior
Describe what you actually observed. Not your interpretation of it. Not what you think they were thinking. Just what happened.

> "...you interrupted Sarah three times while she was explaining the timeline."

### Impact
Explain the effect. On you, on the team, on the work.

> "The client seemed confused about who was leading the project, and Sarah looked frustrated. I'm worried it undermined our credibility."

That's it. No character judgments. No generalizations. Just facts and consequences.

## The Part Most People Mess Up

After delivering the SBI, shut up. Let them respond.

This is where things get real. They might:

- **Agree immediately** — Great, move to problem-solving
- **Get defensive** — Acknowledge their view, then redirect to impact
- **Offer context you didn't have** — Listen. You might learn something.
- **Deny it happened** — Stick to what you observed, don't argue

Whatever they do, stay curious. Ask: "What was going through your mind when that happened?" You might discover the interrupting came from nervousness, not arrogance. That changes how you solve it.

## End Looking Forward

Don't leave the conversation hanging in the problem. Move to action.

> "How can we prevent this in the next client meeting?"
> "What support would help you here?"
> "What do you want to do differently?"

Notice these are questions, not commands. You're collaborating, not dictating. They're more likely to follow through on a solution they helped create.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Waiting too long.** Feedback should land within 48 hours. After that, it feels like an ambush.

**Doing it publicly.** Always private. Always.

**Using "always" or "never."** These words trigger defensiveness instantly because they're almost never accurate.

**Guessing at intent.** You don't know why they did what they did. Ask instead.

**Apologizing for giving feedback.** "I'm sorry to have to tell you this..." Don't. You're helping them.

## The Hardest Truth

Here's what nobody tells you: giving feedback gets easier with practice, but it never feels good. If it did, you'd probably be doing it wrong.

The discomfort you feel? That's you caring about the relationship. Channel it into being thoughtful and specific, not into avoiding the conversation.

Your job isn't to make people feel good in the moment. It's to help them grow. Sometimes that's uncomfortable for everyone.

Do it anyway.
